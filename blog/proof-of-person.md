---
title: "Proof of Person"
subtitle: "On why being real is harder to fake than you think"
date: 2026-05-27
author: James Mason
---

My buddy and I were in a coffee shop talking about AI video, how close it's getting, how soon you won't be able to tell the difference between a real person on screen and a generated one. When anyone can generate a convincing version of you, what actually proves you're real? What's the proof of person?

The obvious answer is verification, credentials, timestamps, or some cryptographic signature that says yes, this was a human who made this. And we'll probably build all of that. But verification answers a different question than trust does. You can prove something exists without ever knowing if it's worth believing in. When most people ask if something is real, they're not asking for a receipt. They're asking whether they can put their weight on it.

Trust gets built differently. Not through what can be certified, but through what can be known: over time, through specificity, contradiction, process, and the willingness to be wrong in public. Not the performance of depth. Actual depth. The kind you only accumulate by living and letting it show.

AI can output extraordinary levels of information and emulation, but sourcing real humanness in real time is perhaps the greatest challenge. It can follow your patterns, pull your context, map your voice, your humor, your take on things, and given enough data, generate a version of you that passes casual inspection. What it can't do is have actually been in the coffee shop. It can't source the non-linear accumulation of a real life: the memories you gained and lost, the experiences that changed you, the moments something felt so serendipitous you couldn't explain it if you tried. The unbothered feeling in one moment, then genuinely ticked off about something small in the next. That rhythm isn't a formula, it's a person.

A real human life doesn't resolve cleanly. It doesn't move in perfectly logical order. It contradicts itself. It's boring and then brilliant and then embarrassingly petty and then inexplicably moved. AI can generate any one of those things, but strung together from actual experience, the pattern has a texture that simulation can't quite get. Something like intuition or instinct. Audiences can feel it, even when they can't say why.

Casey Neistat didn't build trust by being scrappy. He had gear, he had a formula, and he knew what he was doing. But he also let you in. He showed you more than the finished thing: the early wake-ups, the process, the messy middle. That transparency wasn't a style choice. It was evidence. A real person was present when this was made, and he wanted you to know it.

That's what being knowable actually looks like. Not being verifiable, but showing enough of the process, the contradiction, the evolution, that someone can follow the thread. Not the polished version. The version that got there.

I'm still working on this. There's shyness in it, and perfectionism, and a habit of waiting until I've figured something out before putting it out. The instinct to edit before publishing is real, and sometimes it's right. But so is the value of going before you're ready and letting people see the getting there.

Everything AI has ever generated originates downstream from human curiosity, from humanness. It's not the other way around.

Proof of person isn't a new technology problem, it's an old human one. The solution was always the same: be so specifically, honestly, contradictorily yourself that a simulation would have to live your whole life to fake it.

Your instincts. Your contradictions. Your mystery.

That's not something to verify. That's something to witness.
