---
title: "Proof of Person"
subtitle: "On why being real is harder to fake than you think"
date: 2026-05-27
author: James Mason
---

My buddy and I were in a coffee shop talking about AI video, how close it's getting, how soon you won't be able to tell the difference between a real person on screen and a generated one. That sounds scary. When anyone can generate a convincing version of you, what actually proves you're real? What's the proof of person?

The obvious answer is verification, credentials, timestamps, or some cryptographic signature that says yes, this was a human who made this. We'll probably build all of that, but verification answers a different question than trust does. You can prove something exists without knowing if it's worth believing in. When most people ask if something is real, they're not asking for a receipt, they're asking whether they can put their weight on it.

Trust gets built differently. Not only through what can be certified, but through what can be known. Over time, through specificity, raw process, and the willingness to reflect and be accountable, trust can grow. Not the performance of depth, actual depth. The kind you only accumulate by living and letting it show.

AI can output extraordinary levels of information and emulation, but sourcing real humanness in real time is perhaps the greatest challenge. It can follow your patterns, pull your context, map your voice, your humor, your take on things, and given enough data, generate a version of you that passes casual inspection. But can it source the non-linear accumulation of a real human life? The memories you gained and lost, the experiences that changed you, the nuanced stimulus constantly shifting in your environment, subtle or dramatic, the moment something felt so serendipitous you couldn't explain it if you tried. The unbothered feeling in one moment, then genuinely ticked off about something small in the next. That rhythm isn't a formula, it's a person.

A real human life doesn't resolve cleanly. It doesn't move in perfectly logical order, it contradicts itself. It's boring and then brilliant and then embarrassingly petty and then inexplicably moved. AI can generate any one of those things, but strung together from actual experience, the pattern has a texture that simulation can't quite get. Something like intuition or instinct. Audiences can feel it, even when they can't say why.

Casey Neistat built trust by showing his imperfect process. He had gear, he had a formula, and he knew what he was doing. But he also left the mistakes in. He showed you the early wake-ups, the messy middle, the version of things that didn't quite go right. That wasn't accidental. It was a kind of transparency: a real person was present when this was made, and he wanted you to know it. What people connected with wasn't the polish. It was the evidence of a life actually being lived.

That's what being knowable actually looks like. Not only being verifiable, but showing enough of the process, the contradictions, the evolution, that someone can follow the thread. Not the polished version, the version that actually happened.

I'm still working on this. Sometimes my shyness, my fear of being vulnerable, gets in the way of what people can actually connect with. The perfectionism, the habit of waiting until I've figured something out before putting it out there, might be doing me a disservice. The instinct to edit before publishing is natural, and sometimes it's right, but so is the value of going before you're ready and letting people see you get there. Ironically, the very thing we're afraid to show is the very thing that connects us most.

Everything AI has ever generated originates downstream from human curiosity, from humanness. Even the great discoveries, physics, mathematics, art, began with a human wondering why, asking what if. AI is, at its core, a manifestation of that curiosity: built from the patterns of human thought, observation, and imagination. Anything it produces, however extraordinary, is only possible because humans were curious enough to look, to create, to wonder. The creativity, the imagination, the mystery we ponder, that's ours. AI can extend it and accelerate it, but it can't originate it. That already happened.

Proof of person isn't a new technology problem, it's an old human one. The answer isn't new either: be so specifically, honestly, and imperfectly yourself that a simulation would have to live your whole life to fake it.

Your instincts. Your contradictions. Your mystery. The nuance that can't be fully captured because it's always changing.

That's not something merely to verify. It's someone to connect with.
