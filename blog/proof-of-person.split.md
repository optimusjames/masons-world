---
title: "Proof of Person"
subtitle: "On specificity, contradiction, and being unmistakably yourself"
date: 2026-05-27
author: James Mason
---

You can feel when a person actually cared about something they made. It lives in the small choices, the details someone sweated that you'd never consciously notice but would feel the absence of. A curve that took a hundred tries. A sentence that reads easy because it wasn't. The care is the evidence: a real person was here, paying attention, when this was made. That trace is getting harder to trust. A convincing version of almost anyone can be generated now, close enough to pass a glance and getting closer. So the question underneath everything gets louder. When anyone can fake a person, what actually proves one is real?

An answer is verification, credentials, timestamps, or some cryptographic signature that says yes, this was a human who made this. We'll probably build all of that, but verification answers a different question than trust does. You can prove something exists without knowing if it's worth believing in. When most people ask if something is real, they're not asking for a receipt, they're asking whether they can put their weight on it.

AI can emulate you well. It follows your patterns, pulls your context, maps your voice and your humor and your takes, and with enough data it generates a version of you that passes casual inspection. But can it source the whole of an actual life? The memory you lost and the one that won't leave, the serendipity you couldn't explain if you tried, unbothered one moment and ticked off about something small the next. That rhythm isn't a formula, it's a person.

Casey Neistat built trust by showing his imperfect process. He had gear, he had a formula, and he knew what he was doing. But he also left the mistakes in. He showed you the early wake-ups, the messy middle, the version of things that didn't quite go right. That wasn't accidental. It was a kind of transparency: a real person was present when this was made, and he wanted you to know it. What people connected with wasn't the polish. It was the evidence of a life actually being lived.

I'm still working on this. Sometimes shyness shows up, a fear of being seen, and gets in the way of what people could actually connect with. But is it really mine? It feels solid, like a fixed part of who I am. Maybe it isn't. Maybe it's just a feeling passing through, and the solidness is the illusion. What feels most fixed is the least fixed thing about me. Same with the perfectionism, the habit of waiting until I've got something figured out before I put it anywhere. The instinct to edit before publishing is natural, and sometimes it's right. But so is going before you're ready and letting people watch you get there. The thing we're most afraid to show is usually the thing that connects us most.

Proof of person isn't a new technology problem, it's an old human one. The answer isn't new either: be so specifically, honestly, and imperfectly yourself that a simulation would have to live your whole life to fake it.

Your instincts. Your contradictions. Your mystery. The nuance that can't be fully captured because it's always changing.

That's not something merely to verify. It's someone to connect with.
