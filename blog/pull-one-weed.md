subtitle: "On starting before you know what you're doing"

The garden doesn't get easier to think about. You can stand at the window all morning — where the sun hits, what needs water, which corner is a mess. None of it moves a single weed.

Walk outside and pull one. Five minutes later you look up and you're somewhere. The light falls differently than you thought. A plant you forgot about is doing fine. Something else needs attention you couldn't have noticed from inside. The garden tells you what it needs, but only once you start.

That's what starting does. It doesn't just move you forward — it shows you things you couldn't have seen any other way.

Meditation is like this too. It can seem like a lot before you try it — sit still, clear your mind, do it right. But you don't clear your mind. You sit down, take one breath, and just notice. The thoughts rushing through, the restlessness, the quiet that starts to appear underneath. Every time you realize you've drifted and gently come back, that is the practice. The awareness grows through doing it, not through thinking about doing it.

Most things work this way. You start with an idea of what you want to build or where you want to go. That idea is worth having — it gives you a direction. But it's also just a beginning. The doing refines it. You learn things mid-process that no amount of planning ahead could have given you. The plan and the work are always in conversation, each one shaping the next.

We know this. We just forget it every time something feels too big or too undefined to begin.

One weed. One breath. One step.

The rest shows up as you go.
