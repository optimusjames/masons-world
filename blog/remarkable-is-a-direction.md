---
title: "Remarkable Is a Direction, Not a Destination"
subtitle: "On obsession, letting go, and the feeling that tells you it's done"
date: 2026-03-18
author: James Mason
readingTime: 4 min
---

Steve Jobs made the Macintosh team redo the circuit board. Not the case, not the screen. The circuit board. The part no customer would ever see. When someone pushed back, he said a great carpenter doesn't use cheap wood for the back of a cabinet.

That's not perfectionism. That's a direction.

Seth Godin called it the purple cow: the thing so worth remarking on that people can't help themselves. But remarkable doesn't mean flashy. It doesn't mean new. It means something that sticks. Something people didn't realize they needed until it was in front of them. The difference between something impressive and something remarkable is that impressive fades. Remarkable changes what you expect next.

Jobs didn't chase shiny. He chased the feeling of picking something up and knowing, before you'd done anything with it, that someone cared about every part of it. That's not a finish line. You don't arrive at remarkable. You just keep walking toward it, and the walking is the thing.

Getting there takes obsession. But not the grinding, teeth-clenched kind. The kind that ebbs and flows. Structured one day, reckless the next. Tight, then loose. You sketch something clean, then you tear it apart and try something that might be stupid. You let yourself be a little sloppy because the interesting ideas live in the mess. Then you pull it back together.

You don't always have clarity. Most days you don't. You're pushing through fog, testing things, thinking sideways, and nothing connects. Then a small breakthrough hits. Maybe it's a detail that suddenly makes the whole thing click. Maybe it's realizing you've been solving the wrong problem. Either way, it feeds the next push. That's the loop: try, fail, adjust, find a thread, pull it. The breakthroughs come from the oscillation. From zooming in on the details until your eyes cross, then zooming out to ask the real questions. What am I missing? Has this been done? Is this actually what someone needs?

The playfulness matters more than people think. Jobs wasn't grim about this stuff. He was having fun finding the edge. The obsession and the play aren't opposites. The play is what keeps the obsession from collapsing into something mechanical. You need both: the rigor to care about the back of the cabinet, and the looseness to try something nobody asked for.

Here's the part that's hard to talk about: you can't know what the thing is going to be until you put it out there. You can refine forever in your head. You can debate details with yourself until you've talked yourself out of shipping anything. But the thing only becomes real when someone else touches it. There's a fundamental need you're trying to find, something that lasts, something deeper than novelty. And you won't know if you found it until the work is in the world and people tell you what it means to them.

So how do you know when to stop?

Done is a feeling. Not a checklist, not a metric. You know it the way you know a sentence is finished. Something settles. The thing stands on its own and you stop wanting to fix it. You stop debating between two versions of a detail that nobody else will notice.

Jobs shipped the first iPhone without copy-paste. Without copy-paste. That should have felt incomplete. But the thing already landed. He knew. Not because it was perfect. Because it was enough in the right direction.

That's the real skill: knowing the difference between "this needs more work" and "I'm just spinning." When you're spinning, zoom out. Think bigger. Is the thing already what it needs to be? Sometimes the answer was yes three revisions ago.

You don't want to settle. You shouldn't. But there's a version of not-settling that's just avoidance wearing a perfectionist costume. The obsession that got you here has to know when to let go. And once you do, the thing stops being yours. It belongs to whoever finds it. That's not loss. That's the whole point.

Remarkable is a direction. You walk toward it by caring too much, playing too freely, and shipping before you're totally sure. The clarity comes after. It always does.
